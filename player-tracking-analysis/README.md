### Goal 3
![Goal 3](4777_goal.gif)
The final goal of the game is scored by Team USA just 22 seconds into the third period. The sequence begins with the opening faceoff won by Team Canada. Unable to penetrate the American defense, Canada cycles the puck just inside the offensive zone before attempting a dump in. The dump backfires, allowing the United States to regain possession. As the Americans advance toward the offensive zone they turn the puck over, and Canada recovers. Canada attempts a second dump in, which is again collected by the Americans. Using a board pass to gain possession in their own defensive zone, the United States transitions quickly up the ice into the Canadian zone and scores.

### Summary
This game showcases two distinct types of goals: structured plays and transition opportunities. The first goal demonstrates the effectiveness of cycling, a *structured* possession-based approach that creates a scoring chance through patient puck movement. The second and third goals, however, are born from *transitions*, where turnovers and takeaways generate sudden high danger scoring chances. These goals also highlight the risk of dump-ins as a strategy. While these are occasionally useful in calculated situations such as power plays, they consistently surrendered possession in this game, making them a high risk, low reward option. Notably, despite losing the game, the United States won the turnover battle, generating several quality chances through puck recoveries and takeaways. This game is ultimately a compelling example of one of hockey's most important skills, the ability to capitalize on an opponent's mistakes.
